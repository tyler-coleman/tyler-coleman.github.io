hello,

My name is Tyler, and this is my personal website. With time, I hope to grow this into a full-fledged, interactive experience that showcases everything I've worked on and enjoy. For now, I guess this is just a space for me to ramble about myself.

I grew up in the wee town of Cairo, Egypt after moving there with my family at 9 years old. Having been an army brat my whole life prior, moving around wasn't so much an ordeal as it was an inevitability. Nothing, however, could have prepared me for the sometimes awkward but always incredible experience that would be the next 9 years of my life.

I attended Tufts University in Boston following high school. My first Boston winter was about as much of a shock as one would expect for someone who spent their previous 9 worried only about the occasional sandstorms and rain that came with winter in Egypt. In some ways, readjusting to life here was harder than acclimatizing to life on the other side of the planet ever was.

I graduated in 2017 with a B.S. in Electrical Engineering, with cum laude honors. While in my program, I placed a strong emphasis on coding courses and project work. This led to me graduating with a very broad set of technical skills, having written code for all sorts of projects ranging from embedded signal processing systems to web applications to physical systems modeling with Python. I'm at my absolute happiest when learning and working with new technologies.

I'm a musician!

All my life I've been drawn to anything and everything music. From musicals to rock bands to acapella groups, music has always provided the greatest escape in my life. I even got to play the oud in my final year of college with the Arab Music Ensemble at Tufts.

I love to cook (and garden)!

For me, cooking provides a means of catharsis in a way that very few things can. It also provides a similar sort of creatively engaging challenge as the most interesting of engineering problems. It pushes you to better yourself on every attempt. And the end result?

I've wanted to be a pilot since I was a little kid. That dream got shot down pretty early when I learned they don't let colorblind folk fly commercial airplanes. Fair enough, I guess.

Anways,

You'll notice that I dont really have any sort of label for who I am or what I do anywhere here, and the reason for that is simple: I don't really know. I didn't get where I am today by obsessing over the details at every step of my life. Rather, the only thing I've ever really wanted is something to consistently challenge me to grow, to learn, and to have fun in the process. And now I'm in California. Sorry for rambling.

digital guitar effects pedal

(Teensy, embedded C)

I was inspired to do this while working my way through a text on designing audio plugins in C++. The hardware here is pretty simple. An op-amp acting as a buffer biases the input signal from the guitar to 4.5V, then attenuates the signal to 3Vpk-pk before feeding it through an audio adapter to the line input of Cortex M4-based development board. The idea from here is simple - implement whatever DSP algorithms I want and upload the new code to yield whatever guitar effect my heart desires (and my brain can figure out). I'll never spend $200 on a boutique digital delay ever again.

It's pretty ugly, I know.

So far I've managed to implement a simple delay and some filters based on parameters set by potentiometers also being polled in real-time by the DSC. I hope to expand the pedal's capabilities in the future.

fractal generator

(C++, SDL)

This is an interactive desktop app written in C++ and utilizing the SDL graphics library. The program generates a fractal image representing the Mandelbrot set by implementing the escape-time algorithm. The program further implements histogram coloring based on customizable ranges set by the user in main. Each range corresponds to a subset of the histogram, and the colors at the start and end of the ranges are specified using a custom RGB struct. Pixels whose iteration number falls in between the start and end of the range are given a color that is a linear function of the difference between the start and end colors.

To zoom in on a particular area, the user simply clicks the UI at the desired point. A zoom frame is added to an array maintained by the program, and the dimensions of the next frame are calculated before the zoomed image is re-rendered. To return to the last zoom frame, the user simply presses backspace. The max number of iterations executed before the program determines a point falls within the Mandelbrot set is customizable via a constant declared in Mandelbrot.h. Adjusting this constant trades off rendering speed for resolution.

Next, I hope to add elements to the GUI that will allow the user to customize the histogram coloring ranges and iterations per pixel without directly editing the source code. I also hope to optimize the drawing of the fractal by improving on the current implementation of the escape-time algorithm.

Source code at fractal-generator on github.
